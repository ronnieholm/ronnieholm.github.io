--- layout: post title: "2D transformation matrices for translation, scaling, and rotation" date: 2020-03-14 12:00 UTC ---

This post develops the mathematical foundation for the 2D elementary matrices in computer graphics used for translating, scaling, and rotating points. For a hands-on feel for the concepts, the matrices and operations on those are implemented in a graphics application.

We start by defining a traditional 2D coordinate system in terms of (1) orthogonal vectors \(\vec u\) and \(\vec v\), representing \(x\) and \(y\) axis and (2) origin \(o\) representing point of origin, commonly \((0,0)\) where \(\vec u\) and \(\vec v\) intersect. Also, terms point and vector are interchangeably, but when explicitly talking about a point, the notation is to capitalize it. Think of point as a special kind of vector.

A point \(P\) may be defined in terms of vectors \(\vec u\) and \(\vec v\). To arrive at the point from origin, we move some distance along \(\vec u\), then some distance along \(\vec v\), and offsetting with respect to origin \(O\), our point of reference:

$$P = x\vec u + y\vec v + O\\ P = 0.5\vec u + 0.25\vec v + O\\ P = 0.5\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix} + 0.25\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix}\\ P = \begin{bmatrix}0.5\\0\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}0\\0.25\end{bmatrix}\\ P = \begin{bmatrix}0.5\\0.25\end{bmatrix}$$ The fact that \(\vec u\) and \(\vec v\) are unit perpendicular vectors and that \(O\) is the zero vector is convention. More commonly \(P\) is written \((x,y) = (0.5, 0.25)\) where \(x\) and \(y\) are the coordinates of \(P\). Going forward, we'll write \(P\) in matrix form: $$P = \begin{bmatrix}x\ y\ 1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}\vec u\\ \vec v\\ O \end{bmatrix} = x\vec u + y\vec v + O$$ For notational convenience we leave out \(\begin{bmatrix}\vec u\\ \vec v\\ O \end{bmatrix}\) but in terms of the math it's still there. \(P\) in matrix form then becomes \(\begin{bmatrix}x\ y\ 1\end{bmatrix}\). The \(1\) is there to pick up origin \(O\). In order for \(P\) to be a point and not just a vector, we add a point to vector to get a point back.

Translation

Translating a point by \(a\) in \(x\) direction and \(b\) in \(y\) direction may be expressed in terms of original and desired vectors:

$$\begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1\end{bmatrix} \rightarrow \begin{bmatrix}x + a & y + b & 1\end{bmatrix}$$

Transforming the vector on the left into the vector on the right means applying an operator \(T_{a,b}\) to the left side. The operator takes in any \(x\) and \(y\) and translates it by \(a\) and \(b\). Expanding the multiplication enables us to verify correctness of \(T_{a,b}\) matrix: $$\begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 & 0\\0 & 1 & 0\\a & b & 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1x + 0y + 1a & 0x + 1y + 1b & 0x + 0y + 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}x + a & y + b & 1\end{bmatrix}$$ Two characteristics of the transformation matrices we'll encounter is that they're sparse and that the last column is always the same to pick up origin \(O\) and carry it over unchanged.

Scaling

Scaling a point by \(a\) in \(x\) direction and \(b\) in \(y\) direction has the effect of shrinking (\(a < 1\)) or expanding (\(a > 1\)) with respect to origin. Expressed in terms of vectors:

$$\begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1 \end{bmatrix}\rightarrow \begin{bmatrix}ax & by & 1\end{bmatrix}$$

We define a scaling matrix \(S_{a, b}\) as $$\begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1\end{bmatrix}\rightarrow \begin{bmatrix}a & 0 & 0\\ 0 & b & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}ax & by & 1\end{bmatrix}$$ Scaling only works when the center of the shape is at origin \(O\). This would be the case for a unit square with control points in \((-1,1)\), \((1,1)\), \((1,-1)\), and \((-1,-1)\), and scaling from a \(2 x 2\) to a \(4 x 4\) square is equal to applying \(S_{2, 2}\) to each control of the square.

But if instead the unit square has control points \((1,3)\), \((3,3)\), \((3,1)\), and \((1,1)\), then center becomes \(2, 2\) and \(S_{2, 2}\) would scale as well as move the center. In that case, each point relative to the square's center of mass must be translated to origin, then scaled, and then translated back.

$$T_{-2,-2} S_{2,2} T_{2,2}$$

For the simple unit square, determining the center is easy. For a general polygon, we'd have to calculate its center of mass.

Rotation

Rotating a point \(\theta\) around origin implies that distance \(r\), radius, between origin and \((x, y)\) and between origin and \((x', y')\) doesn't change: $$\begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1\end{bmatrix} \rightarrow \begin{bmatrix}x' & y' & 1\end{bmatrix}$$ With radius \(r\) and angle \(\phi\) between the \(x\) axis and point, current position is $$x = r\cos\phi\\ y = r\sin\phi$$ The target point is then the current position \(x\) and \(y\) with the additional rotation by \(\theta\): $$\begin{eqnarray*} x' = r\cos(\phi + \theta) & & \\ x' = r\cos \phi \cos \theta - r \sin \phi \sin \theta & & \textrm{\{apply identity sum: \(\cos(u + v) = \cos u \cos v - \sin u \sin v\)\}}\\ x' = x \cos \phi - y \sin \theta & & \textrm{\{substitute in definitions of \(x\) and \(y\)\}}\\\\ y' = r\sin(\phi + \theta) & & \\ y' = r \sin \phi \cos \theta + r \cos \phi \sin \theta & & \textrm{\{apply identity sum: \(\sin(u + v) = \sin u \cos v + \cos u \sin v\)\}}\\ y' = y \cos \theta + x \sin \theta & & \textrm{\{substitute in definitions of \(x\) and \(y\)\}} \end{eqnarray*}$$ Rewriting to matrix form, rotation matrix \(R_\theta\) becomes $$\begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}\cos\theta & \sin\theta & 0\\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}x' & y' & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$ As with scaling, rotation around origin is assumed. If that isn't the case, then we must first translate, then scale, then translate: $$T_{-a,-b} R_\theta T_{a, b}$$

Summary

Collectively, these matrices are known as the elementary matrices in computer graphics. Instead of applying these transformations one at a time to a point, transformation matrices may be multiplied together to a single matrix \(T\) applied to a point: $$\begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1\end{bmatrix} T \begin{bmatrix}\vec u\\ \vec v\\ O \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}x' & y' & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$
